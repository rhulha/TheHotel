Four




Nate was at the door at five-fifty.

Day three, and the routine was starting to feel less like a test he might fail and more like something he could actually do. His suit was pressed. His shoes were polished. He'd drunk one cup of coffee instead of two, which meant his hands weren't shaking.

At six o'clock exactly, the lock clicked.

David opened the door and gave him the smallest of nods. "Good morning."

"Good morning."

They moved through the morning routine in near silence. Nate checked the ledger while David reviewed the overnight logs. Three checkouts. Two arrivals scheduled for after noon. One VIP arrival at eleven—marked in red in the ledger, which meant Nate should probably stay out of the lobby at eleven.

He shadowed David through the room inspections. 304 was clean. 511 was clean. 628 had a bottle of vodka in the mini-bar that hadn't been there yesterday, which David noted in a small leather notebook before photographing the bottle with his phone.

"Guest forgot to report it?" Nate asked.

"Or didn't want to." David pocketed his phone. "Either way, it goes in the log."

At seven-fifteen, Rebecca was waiting for him in the restaurant. She looked tired today, Nate thought. Not exhausted, but like she'd been dealing with something annoying for too long.

"Table assignments are the same as yesterday," she said, handing him the chart. "Breakfast service until nine-thirty, then you're on rotation with Sophie."

"Rotation?"

"Guest services. Whatever needs doing." She walked toward the kitchen. "Don't screw up the coffee this time."

Nate had poured coffee into a water glass yesterday. Once. It had been a long morning.

The breakfast service went smoothly. He seated eight guests without mixing up a single table number. He poured coffee into coffee cups and water into water glasses. Mrs. Delacroix arrived at seven forty-five and he had her oatmeal and black coffee ready before she sat down.

At nine-thirty, Sophie appeared in the doorway.

"Ready?" she asked.

"For what?"

"You'll see. Come on."

They took the elevator to the second floor. As the numbers lit up—one, two—Nate noticed Sophie was wearing her key card on a red lanyard today instead of the usual blue one.

"New lanyard?" he asked.

Sophie glanced down at it. "Oh. Yeah. Red means I'm authorized for secure areas today. We're doing a deep clean on three."

"What's on three?"

"Nothing now. But there was a conference yesterday, which means we sweep for bugs and reset everything." The elevator doors opened. "You did good finding that transmitter the other day. Today you're going to learn what we do when we find one."

They walked down the hallway to a conference room identical to the one Nate had helped Sophie prep two days ago. She unlocked the door and flipped on the lights.

The room looked untouched. Chairs perfectly aligned. Table polished to a mirror shine. Not a single glass or notepad left behind.

"Looks clean," Nate said.

"It always does." Sophie pulled out the RF detector from her bag. "That's the point."

They swept the room together. Nate took the perimeter while Sophie checked the furniture. The detector stayed quiet until Sophie moved it near the sideboard, where it started clicking faster.

"Got something," she said.

Nate watched as she ran the detector along the underside of the coffee service. The clicking accelerated into a steady whine. She pulled out a small flashlight and examined the bottom of the silver coffee pot.

"There." She showed him a disc identical to the one they'd found before. Flat, black, barely larger than a quarter. "Someone planted this yesterday during the conference."

"During?"

"Probably. Which means someone in that meeting wanted to record it." She peeled the transmitter off carefully and dropped it into a plastic evidence bag. "This goes to Mr. Carver. He'll figure out who it belongs to."

"And then what?"

Sophie looked at him. "Then nothing. We found it, we logged it, and we forget about it. That's the job."

They finished sweeping the room and found two more transmitters—one behind a painting, one taped to the underside of a chair. Sophie bagged all three and made notes in a small ledger.

"Three in one room," Nate said. "Is that normal?"

"Nothing here is normal. But yes, conferences tend to attract listeners." She locked the conference room behind them. "Come on. We've got another job."

They took the elevator down to the lobby. As the doors opened, Nate saw movement near the private elevator.

He remembered David's warning. Don't look. Don't listen. Don't remember.

He looked at the floor.

Sophie stepped out first and Nate followed, keeping his eyes down. He heard voices—low, professional, the kind of tones that suggested people who were used to being obeyed. Footsteps on marble. The soft chime of the private elevator.

"Nate." Sophie's voice was quiet. "Come on."

He followed her toward the stairs, still not looking up.

When they reached the stairwell door, Sophie stopped and glanced back at the lobby. "You're learning."

"David made it very clear."

"Good." She pushed open the door. "Because that was the mayor."

Nate almost turned to look back.

"Don't," Sophie said. "You didn't see him. You don't know he was here. And you definitely don't mention it to anyone."

"Got it."

They took the stairs down to the basement. It was cooler here, and the walls were exposed brick. Sophie led him through a maze of hallways that all looked identical—rough plaster, bare bulbs in wire cages, pipes running along the ceiling.

"How do you not get lost down here?" Nate asked.

"Practice." She stopped at a door marked "Storage" and unlocked it. "Also, maps. There's a master layout in David's office if you ever need it."

The storage room was larger than Nate expected. Shelves lined the walls, packed with linens, supplies, cleaning equipment, and boxes labeled with dates going back decades. A workbench sat in the center of the room, tools arranged with military precision.

Martin was at the bench, working on what looked like a phone system. He glanced up as they entered.

"Sophie. Kid." He set down a screwdriver. "What brings you to my domain?"

"Need to drop these off." Sophie handed him the evidence bags with the transmitters. "Three from conference room B. All planted within the last twenty-four hours."

Martin took the bags and examined the contents through the plastic. "Someone was serious about listening in."

"Conference was red-flagged," Sophie said. "Carver will want to know."

"I'll handle it." Martin set the bags aside and looked at Nate. "How's the job treating you, kid?"

"Still here."

"That's something. Most new hires don't make it past day two." He pulled out a clipboard and made a note. "You find these?"

"Sophie found them. I just watched."

"Good training." Martin checked his watch. "You two got a minute? I could use an extra set of hands with something."

Sophie glanced at Nate. "You good?"

"Yeah."

"All right. What do you need?"

Martin led them deeper into the basement, past the storage room and down another hallway. The air got colder. The walls changed from plaster to old brick, the kind that looked like it had been there since the building was constructed.

"How far down does this place go?" Nate asked.

"Far enough." Martin stopped at a heavy steel door with a digital keypad. "This is the secure storage area. We keep certain items down here that guests leave behind or request we hold for them."

He punched in a code and the door clicked open. Inside was a small room lined with safety deposit boxes, a few larger steel lockers, and a table with a locked box sitting on it.

"Guest in 812 requested secure storage for this," Martin said, gesturing at the box. "I need to inventory the contents, log it, and get it into one of the lockers. Company policy says I need two witnesses."

Sophie moved to one side of the table. Nate took the other.

Martin pulled on a pair of latex gloves and unlocked the box. Inside was a leather portfolio, three flash drives, a bundle of documents wrapped in plastic, and what looked like a very expensive watch.

He photographed each item, made notes on a form, then carefully transferred everything to a locker marked "812-A."

"That's it?" Nate asked.

"That's it." Martin stripped off the gloves. "Simple job, but it has to be witnessed. You two sign here."

They signed the form. Martin locked the locker and led them back out to the hallway.

"Thanks for the help," he said. "Sophie, you know where to find me if you need anything. Kid—" He pointed at Nate. "You're doing good. Keep your head down and your mouth shut, and you might actually last here."

"That's the goal."

"We'll see."

Sophie and Nate made their way back through the basement maze. Nate tried to memorize the turns—left at the boiler room, right at the electrical panel, straight past the laundry—but he knew he'd be lost if he tried it on his own.

"How long have you been working here?" Nate asked.

"Three years. Why?"

"Just trying to figure out how long it takes before all this feels normal."

Sophie laughed. "It never feels normal. You just get used to feeling like you don't know what's going on."

They reached the stairs and started climbing. Halfway to the first floor, Sophie stopped.

"Nate. Can I give you some advice?"

"Sure."

"You're doing really well. Better than most new hires. But I can see you getting curious about things." She looked at him with an expression that was part concern, part warning. "Don't. I mean it. This place has secrets, and some of them are dangerous. The people who last here are the ones who learn what they need to know and ignore everything else."

"Like what?"

"Like the private elevator. Like the rooms on ten. Like why the mayor shows up once a week and takes the elevator down instead of up."

Nate felt something cold settle in his stomach. "Down?"

Sophie's expression didn't change. "You didn't hear that from me."

"I didn't hear anything."

"Good." She started climbing again. "Come on. We've got another floor to check before lunch."

They reached the first floor and Sophie's phone buzzed. She checked it and sighed.

"Change of plans. David needs you in the lobby. I've got to handle something on six."

"What does David need?"

"No idea. But when David calls, you go." She headed for the elevator. "Good luck."

Nate walked into the lobby and found David at the front desk, writing in the ledger. David looked up as he approached.

"You're on door duty until eleven. Then you're shadowing me for a special arrival."

"The red-flag arrival?"

"That's the one." David closed the ledger. "This is your first VIP. You're going to see things. Important people. People you might recognize. You're going to stand there, keep your mouth shut, and do exactly what I tell you. Clear?"

"Clear."

"I hope so. Because this is the kind of guest who can end your career with a phone call if they think you're a problem."

Nate took his position by the door and waited. For the next hour, he opened doors for guests, greeted them professionally, and tried not to think about what kind of person could end his career with a phone call.

At ten fifty-five, David appeared beside him.

"VIP arrives in five minutes. When they come through that door, you keep your eyes down. You do not greet them. You do not offer assistance. You do not exist. Understood?"

"Understood."

At eleven o'clock exactly, three black SUVs pulled up outside.

Nate kept his eyes on the marble floor.

He heard car doors opening. Multiple sets of footsteps on the sidewalk. The front door opened—someone else was handling it, Nate realized. David had moved him away from the entrance.

More footsteps on marble. Professional voices giving quiet instructions.

"Clear."

"Elevator is ready."

"Let's move."

Nate counted at least eight people crossing the lobby. Maybe more. He heard the private elevator chime—that deep, resonant sound—and then the doors closing.

Silence.

"All right," David said. "You did good. That was a senator, his security detail, and three people I'm not going to tell you about. You didn't see them. You don't know they were here. And if anyone asks—press, friends, family—you've never heard of them. Yes?"

"Yes."

"Go help Rebecca with lunch prep. Your shift ends at two."

Nate walked toward the restaurant, his mind spinning with questions he knew better than to ask.

Rebecca was setting tables when he arrived. She looked up and smiled—an actual smile, which was rare.

"Heard you handled your first VIP."

"Word travels fast."

"It's a small hotel." She handed him a stack of menus. "You did good. David said you kept your eyes down the whole time."

"He told me to."

"Most people can't help themselves. They get curious." She started folding napkins with practiced efficiency. "You want to last here, you learn to kill that curiosity real fast."

"That's what Sophie said."

"Sophie's smart. Listen to her." Rebecca glanced toward the door. "Speaking of which, she wanted to see you during your break. Said to meet her in the break room at noon."

"Where's the break room?"

"First floor, back hallway past the kitchen. You can't miss it."

At noon, Nate found the break room tucked away at the end of a hallway he'd walked past a dozen times without noticing. It was small—a table, four chairs, a counter with a coffee maker and a mini-fridge. Sophie was sitting at the table with another guy Nate hadn't met yet.

The guy was in his late twenties, with dark hair and the kind of casual confidence that suggested he'd been working here long enough to stop being nervous. He looked up as Nate entered and gave him an appraising look.

"You're the new guy."

"Nate."

"Marcus." He gestured at the empty chair. "Sophie said you're doing all right."

"Trying."

Sophie poured herself a coffee and sat down. "Marcus has been here for five years. He works night shift, so you probably haven't seen him around."

"Night shift?"

"Six p.m. to two a.m." Marcus leaned back in his chair. "Different world at night. Quieter. Weirder."

Nate sat down. "Weirder how?"

Marcus and Sophie exchanged a look.

"Nate," Sophie said carefully, "Marcus and I have been talking, and we think you should know some things. Things David and Rebecca aren't going to tell you."

"About the hotel?"

"About what happens here." Marcus pulled out his phone, checked something, then set it face-down on the table. "You've been here three days. You've seen the private elevator. You've seen the VIP guests. You've probably noticed that this place operates on a completely different level than any normal hotel."

"I've noticed."

"Good. Because you need to understand something." Marcus leaned forward. "This hotel doesn't just cater to powerful people. It protects them. The police don't investigate what happens here. The city looks the other way. Even the mayor does what the hotel wants."

"How do you know that?"

Sophie spoke up. "Because I've seen it. A friend of mine worked here last year. Elena. She was on night shift with Marcus. One night she saw something she wasn't supposed to see—some guests doing something in one of the conference rooms. She didn't even ask questions. Just saw it and left."

"What happened to her?"

"She disappeared." Sophie's voice was flat. "Didn't show up for her next shift. Didn't answer her phone. I went to the police, and they told me there was nothing to investigate. Said she probably just quit and moved. But her apartment was still full of her stuff. Her car was still parked outside. She didn't quit. She just vanished."

Nate felt that cold feeling in his stomach again. "Did you go to the police again?"

"Three times," Marcus said. "They refused to investigate. Wouldn't even file a missing persons report. The detective I talked to basically told me to drop it if I wanted to keep my job."

"Jesus."

"Yeah." Marcus drummed his fingers on the table. "That's when I learned how deep this goes. The hotel has connections everywhere. Police, city hall, probably state government too. If they want something to go away, it goes away."

Sophie wrapped her hands around her coffee cup. "I'm not telling you this to scare you. I'm telling you this so you understand what you're dealing with. This job pays well because they're buying your silence. And your compliance."

"Why are you still here?" Nate asked.

"Because the money's good and I have student loans," Sophie said. "And because leaving wouldn't bring Elena back."

"I'm here because I'm curious," Marcus said. "I want to know what's really going on in this place. What they're hiding. Why they need this level of secrecy."

"Have you found anything?"

Marcus smiled, but it didn't reach his eyes. "Some things. Little pieces. Nothing that makes sense yet." He glanced at Sophie. "But we're working on it."

"Working on what?"

"Figuring out what's in the basement," Sophie said quietly. "Below the storage level. There are areas down there that require special clearance. Areas that even Martin doesn't have access to. And the private elevator goes down there. We've seen it."

Nate thought about what Sophie had said earlier. The mayor takes the elevator down instead of up.

"What do you think is down there?" he asked.

"That's what we're trying to find out," Marcus said. "And here's where you come in. You're new. You're fresh. David and Carver don't have you tagged as someone who asks questions yet. You can move around, pay attention, and maybe notice things that Sophie and I can't get close to anymore."

"You want me to spy for you?"

"I want you to keep your eyes open," Marcus said. "And if you see something interesting, you tell us. That's all."

Sophie leaned forward. "Look, you don't have to do anything. You can forget we had this conversation and just do your job and collect your paycheck. Nobody would blame you. But if you're the kind of person who needs to know what's really going on—if you're curious about why a hotel has this much power and this much secrecy—then we can help each other."

Nate looked between them. Sophie, who'd been nothing but helpful since his first day. Marcus, who he'd just met but who seemed genuinely angry about what had happened to their friend.

"I need to think about it," he said.

"Fair enough." Marcus stood and grabbed his phone. "I've got to get some sleep before tonight's shift. Sophie has my number if you want to talk." He headed for the door, then stopped and turned back. "One more thing. Be careful around Carver. He's smart, and he can read people. If you decide to help us, you need to be absolutely sure you can keep it off your face when he's watching."

He left.

Sophie stood and poured out her coffee in the sink. "I meant what I said. You don't have to do anything. But I wanted you to know the truth about this place. About what happened to Elena. About what kind of people we're really working for."

"Thanks for telling me."

"Just be careful, Nate. This job can be good for you if you keep your head down. But if you start asking questions..." She trailed off. "Just be careful."

She left, and Nate sat alone in the break room, staring at the wall and trying to process everything he'd just heard.

A friend disappeared. The police refused to investigate. The hotel had power over the city.

And somewhere in the basement, there was something important enough to require the mayor to visit in person.

Nate checked his watch. One-fifteen. Forty-five minutes left in his shift.

He stood, threw away his untouched coffee, and headed back to work.

His mind was racing, but his face was calm.

He was learning.

At two p.m., David met him at the front desk.

"Good work today. Tomorrow, five-fifty at the door."

"I'll be there."

Nate walked out into the afternoon sunlight and realized his third day was over. He'd survived another VIP arrival. He'd learned more about the hotel's secrets. And he'd been invited to join something that felt both important and incredibly dangerous.

He pulled out his phone and saw a text from Sophie.

No pressure. But if you decide you want to know more, we're here.

Nate pocketed the phone and started walking toward his car.

Tomorrow, he thought. Tomorrow he'd figure out what to do.

He had no idea that tomorrow, he'd see the door to the basement levels.

And everything would change.
