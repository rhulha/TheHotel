Krabat (1971) by Otfried Preußler is a German young-adult fantasy novel, based on a Sorbian (Wendish) folk legend from Lusatia. Here's what happens:

Setup
Krabat is a 14-year-old orphan beggar boy in 17th-century Saxony (around the time of the Thirty Years' War). He's drawn by recurring dreams — a voice calling him — to a watermill in the Koselbruch, a marsh near the village of Schwarzkollm. He becomes an apprentice there.

The dark secret
The mill isn't really a mill. The Master (der Meister) secretly runs a school of black magic, and the twelve journeymen (including Krabat) are his pupils. They learn sorcery from the Koraktor, a book of black magic. But there's a terrible price:

Once a year, on New Year's Eve, one of the journeymen must die so the Master can keep his own pact and stay alive. The deaths look like accidents.
The number is always kept at twelve: when one dies, a new boy is lured to the mill to replace him.
The Master himself is bound to a mysterious, sinister figure — the Gevatter ("Goodman" / the man with the cockfeather), essentially a Death/Devil figure who arrives at the new moon.
Krabat slowly realizes that his friend Tonda, the kind head journeyman, dies on New Year's Eve — and later his friend Michal dies too. He understands the cycle and that he may be next.

The escape / climax
Krabat falls in love with a village girl — the Kantorka (a girl who sings in the church choir). This love becomes his way out. The Master offers Krabat the chance to become his successor, but Krabat refuses.

The way to break the spell: the girl who loves you must come to the mill and correctly identify you among the twelve ravens/journeymen (the Master turns them indistinguishable), asking for your freedom — but she must do it through love, knowing that if she chooses wrong, she dies, and so does Krabat.

The Kantorka comes. The Master tries to trick her, but she senses Krabat's fear for her — it's his love and fear for her safety (not his own) that lets her pick him out correctly. This breaks the Master's power; the Master dies, the mill's magic is destroyed, and the surviving journeymen are freed — though they lose their magical powers. Krabat and the others walk away free, into an ordinary but real life.
