Two




Nate was outside the Hotel at five-thirty.

He knew it was excessive. He knew showing up half an hour early made him look desperate. But desperate was exactly what he was, and right now he'd take looking desperate over being late on his first day.

The street was quieter than it had been yesterday afternoon. The early morning light turned the limestone facade from gray to something close to gold. A delivery truck rumbled past, and somewhere a few blocks over a car alarm went off and then cut itself short. Nate stood on the opposite side of the street and watched the Hotel's entrance like it might disappear if he looked away.

He'd been awake since three a.m., lying in the darkness of his soon-to-be-former apartment, running through every scenario he could think of. What if he said the wrong thing? What if he walked in on something he wasn't supposed to see? What if that whole speech about discretion and his "continued good health" wasn't just corporate intimidation?

At four a.m. he'd given up on sleep and showered. His one good suit—the same one he'd worn to the interview—hung on the back of his door, freshly pressed. He'd borrowed an iron from his neighbor, the one who was always complaining about the noise. She'd given him a look that said she knew he was about to stop being her problem, and handed it over without a word.

By four-thirty he was dressed. By five he'd drunk two cups of instant coffee and checked his wallet three times to make sure he had his ID and banking information. By five-fifteen he was in his car, a fifteen-year-old Honda Civic that burned oil and made a noise like a dying cat whenever he turned left.

Now he stood across the street, watching the minutes crawl by on his phone. Five thirty-five. Five forty. Five forty-five.

At five fifty-five, he crossed the street.

The door was locked.

Nate tried the handle again, more carefully this time, as if maybe he'd missed something. The brass was cold under his palm, and the door didn't budge. He stepped back and looked up at the building. No lights visible in any windows. No movement. Nothing.

His stomach dropped. Had he gotten the time wrong? Had Carver said six p.m. and not six a.m.? No, that didn't make sense. Morning. Six a.m. Bring two forms of ID. He was certain.

At five fifty-eight, he tried the door again.

At five fifty-nine, he considered sitting on the steps and waiting.

At six o'clock exactly, he heard the lock click.

The door swung inward, and a young man in a dark suit identical to Nate's looked out at him. The man was maybe twenty-five, with sharp features and dark eyes that took in every detail of Nate in about two seconds.

"Jennings?" the man asked.

"Yes. I'm—"

"You're on time. Good." The man stepped aside and gestured for Nate to enter. "I'm David Wei. I run the day shift. You're with me for the next two weeks."

Nate stepped into the lobby. It looked different in the early morning light filtering through the high windows. The chandeliers were off. The fireplace was still cold. The marble floor gleamed like it had been polished overnight, which it probably had.

David closed the door and locked it with a key from his pocket. "First thing you need to know," he said, not looking at Nate, "is that punctuality isn't about being on time. It's about being ready when you're needed. Six a.m. means you're inside at six a.m., ready to work. Not standing outside trying the door."

"The door was locked."

"It unlocks at six." David walked toward the mahogany desk, his shoes clicking on the marble. "Which means you should've been here early enough to be waiting when it opened."

Nate followed him. "I was here at five-thirty."

David stopped and turned. For the first time, something like amusement flickered across his face. It didn't look friendly. "Across the street doesn't count. Next time, you wait at the door. Five-fifty is appropriate. Five-fifty-five is acceptable. Six o'clock is late."

It felt like a test he'd already failed, but Nate nodded. "Understood."

"We'll see." David moved behind the desk and pulled out a leather-bound book identical to the one Mrs. Chen had been writing in yesterday. He opened it, scanned a page, and closed it again. "Mrs. Chen handles the night shift. She leaves at six. I take over until two p.m. During those hours, you do what I tell you, when I tell you, exactly how I tell you. Questions?"

"No."

"Yes, you have questions. Everyone does on their first day. You'll keep them to yourself unless they're about a task I've given you. I don't have time to explain why we do things a certain way. You'll learn by watching and doing. Clear?"

"Clear."

David studied him for another moment, then nodded. "Your paperwork is in the office. We'll handle that at seven. Right now, you're going to shadow me. Stay close, pay attention, and don't touch anything unless I tell you to."

The next hour was a blur of rooms and routines that Nate tried desperately to memorize.

David showed him the desk system—a combination of the leather-bound ledger, a computer that looked like it belonged in a museum, and a color-coded filing system that made no sense until David explained that red meant VIP, blue meant extended stay, and green meant staff should not engage unless absolutely necessary.

"Green means what?" Nate asked.

David gave him a look that suggested he'd already forgotten the rule about questions. "It means you see them, you nod, and you move on. You don't offer assistance. You don't make small talk. You definitely don't ask if there's anything you can help them with. They're here for privacy, and you're here to be invisible."

"Got it."

They moved through the first floor, David pointing out details with the efficiency of someone who'd given this tour a hundred times and wanted it to be the hundred and first. The restaurant entrance was through a hallway to the left of the lobby. Kitchen access was staff-only, through a door that required a key card. The elevator—there were two, Nate noticed, not one—required a different key card to access certain floors.

"How many floors are there?" Nate asked.

"Ten." David didn't slow down. "Floors one through five are standard luxury accommodations. Six through eight are suites. Nine is executive suites. Ten is private."

"Private meaning—"

"Private meaning you don't go up there unless Mr. Carver personally tells you to. Which he won't, because you're new and unproven."

They took the stairs—"Faster than waiting for the elevator and you should know where they are"—to the second floor. The hallway was wide and carpeted with the same Persian runners Nate had seen yesterday. Brass room numbers were mounted beside each door. David stopped at Room 204 and pulled out a key card.

"Guest checked out at midnight. Housekeeping won't start until seven. Before they do, you're going to do a walkthrough with me."

He unlocked the door and stepped inside.

The room was immaculate. King bed with white linens that looked crisp enough to cut. Dark wood furniture that matched the aesthetic of the lobby. Heavy curtains drawn across windows that Nate guessed offered a view of something expensive. A bottle of scotch sat on the sideboard next to two crystal glasses.

David walked through the room like he was conducting an inspection. He checked the bathroom, the closet, under the bed. He opened the scotch and sniffed it, then screwed the cap back on. He pulled back the curtains and examined the window latches.

"What are you looking for?" Nate asked.

"Anything that shouldn't be here." David moved to the bed and pulled back the linens, examining the mattress. "Bugs, recording devices, cameras, contraband, evidence of a crime. You'd be surprised what people leave behind."

"Have you found anything?"

"Twice." David didn't elaborate. He finished his inspection and nodded. "Clean. You'll learn to do this in under three minutes. Come on."

They did two more rooms. By the third one, Nate started to understand the pattern. Check the bathroom first—medicine cabinet, shower, toilet tank. Then the closet. Then the bed. Then the furniture, especially anything with drawers. Then the windows.

On the fourth room, David stepped aside. "Your turn."

Nate's pulse kicked up. He stepped into Room 312 and started with the bathroom. Medicine cabinet was empty. Shower was clean. He checked the toilet tank—

"Wrong," David said from the doorway.

Nate turned. "What?"

"You checked the tank before the bowl."

"Does it matter?"

"Everything matters. If there's something in the bowl, you need to know before you stick your hand in the tank. Do it again."

Nate did it again. Then he moved to the closet. Empty hangers. Nothing on the floor. Nothing on the shelf.

He went to the bed and pulled back the linens. The mattress looked fine. He checked under the bed—

"Slower," David said.

Nate looked back at him. "What?"

"You're rushing. If something's under there, you'll miss it. Use the flashlight."

"I don't have a flashlight."

David pulled a small Mag-Lite from his jacket pocket and handed it over. "Now you do. Again."

Nate checked under the bed again, slower this time, sweeping the flashlight beam across the carpet. Nothing.

He moved to the dresser and opened the drawers. Empty. He checked the sideboard. A bottle of wine, unopened. Two glasses. A corkscrew.

He moved to the windows and checked the latches.

"Time," David said.

"What?"

"Five minutes, forty seconds. You need to cut that in half."

"How?"

"Practice." David took the flashlight back. "But you've got the basics. Most new hires forget to check under the bed. Or they skip the windows."

It was the closest thing to praise Nate had gotten all morning, and he decided to take it.

By seven a.m., they were back in the office—a small room behind the front desk that Nate hadn't seen yesterday. David handed him a stack of forms that looked like they'd been drafted sometime in the 1950s and hadn't been updated since.

"Fill these out. Don't lie. We already know the answers."

Nate sat at a small desk in the corner and started working through the paperwork. Tax forms. Direct deposit information. Emergency contact—he left that one blank. David noticed.

"No one?"

"No one."

David studied him for a moment, then nodded. "That's fine. It's better, actually."

"Better how?"

"Fewer complications." David took the completed forms and filed them in a cabinet. "You'll get your uniform tomorrow. Until then, that suit is acceptable. Keep it clean."

"I only have the one."

"Then get it dry-cleaned tonight. There's a place two blocks east that we use. Tell them you're from the Hotel. They'll have it ready by tomorrow morning." David checked his watch. "Seven-fifteen. Breakfast service starts at seven-thirty. You're working the floor with Rebecca."

"Who's Rebecca?"

"You'll find out."

The restaurant was at the back of the first floor, through a set of double doors that opened into a space that looked like it belonged in a European manor. High ceilings. More chandeliers. Tables set with white linens and silver that caught the light from the tall windows overlooking a courtyard Nate hadn't known existed.

A woman in her mid-twenties stood near the kitchen doors, counting silverware. She had dark hair pulled into a bun and sharp eyes that reminded Nate of David. When she saw him, those eyes narrowed.

"You're the new guy."

"Nate Jennings."

"Rebecca Park. You're with me until nine. Don't screw up." She handed him a stack of menus. "You're seating and clearing. I'm taking orders and managing the kitchen. Guests start arriving in fifteen minutes. You seat them, give them menus, pour water, and get out of the way. When I bring food, you clear the previous course. Don't talk unless spoken to. Don't make eye contact unless necessary. Be invisible."

"David said the same thing."

"Then maybe you'll remember it." She turned and headed for the kitchen. "Set up table six. It's two top by the window."

Nate looked around. He counted five windows.

Rebecca stopped and looked back. "Problem?"

"Which window?"

She pointed. "That one. The table with two chairs. We call that a two top. The one with four chairs is a four top. The one with six chairs is—"

"A six top. Got it."

"Good. Now set it up."

Nate approached the table and realized he had no idea what "set it up" meant. There were already plates and silverware arranged on the white tablecloth. He looked back at Rebecca.

She was watching him with an expression that suggested she was enjoying this.

"The silverware is backwards," she said.

Nate looked at the table. Fork on the right, knife on the left.

"Backwards," Rebecca repeated.

He switched them. Fork on the left, knife on the right.

"Better. Now fix the water glasses."

He looked at the water glasses. They seemed fine.

"They're not aligned with the knife tips," Rebecca said.

Nate adjusted the glasses until they were lined up exactly with the tips of the knives.

"Now check the napkins."

The napkins were folded into some kind of origami shape that Nate couldn't begin to replicate. He looked at Rebecca.

"If you have to redo a napkin, there are extras in the sideboard. Second drawer. Don't unfold a guest's napkin unless it's dirty or damaged. These are fine."

"How do you know?"

"Because I did them an hour ago." She checked her watch. "Seven-twenty-eight. First guest arrives in two minutes. Stand by the door and wait."

Nate positioned himself near the double doors and tried not to look nervous. At exactly seven-thirty, the doors opened and a man in his sixties entered. He wore a suit that probably cost more than Nate's car and had the kind of posture that suggested he expected the world to accommodate him.

Nate stepped forward. "Good morning, sir. Table for—"

"I have a reservation," the man said, not looking at him. "Hendricks."

Nate glanced back at Rebecca. She was writing something down and didn't look up.

"Of course, Mr. Hendricks. If you'll follow me."

He led the man to a four top near the center of the room. The man stopped.

"I requested the corner table."

Nate looked at the corner. There was a two top there, already set. "I apologize, sir. Let me—"

"Nate." Rebecca appeared beside him with a leather-bound book that looked like a smaller version of the one at the front desk. "Mr. Hendricks is at table twelve. Corner window."

She led the man to the corner four top that Nate had somehow missed entirely. Hendricks sat without thanking her. Rebecca placed a menu in front of him, poured water from a silver pitcher that seemed to materialize from nowhere, and gestured for Nate to follow her back to the kitchen doors.

"Table twelve is the corner four top," she said quietly. "Table six is the window two top. Table nine is the courtyard four top. You'll have this memorized by tomorrow."

"How many tables are there?"

"Sixteen."

The next hour and a half was a masterclass in controlled chaos.

Guests arrived in a steady stream. Nate seated them, or tried to. He got the table numbers wrong twice. He poured water into a coffee cup. He handed a menu to a guest who'd been coming to the Hotel for twenty years and apparently had the menu memorized.

Rebecca corrected him each time with the kind of patience that felt more dangerous than yelling.

"Water goes in water glasses. Coffee goes in coffee cups."

"Mrs. Delacroix doesn't need a menu. She has oatmeal and black coffee every morning."

"Don't hover. Seat them and step away."

By eight-thirty, Nate's shirt was sticking to his back and his feet hurt from standing. By nine, he'd developed a nervous habit of checking and rechecking the table numbers on a small chart Rebecca had finally given him.

At nine-fifteen, a woman in her late twenties walked into the restaurant and Rebecca's entire demeanor changed.

"Sophie. You're early."

The woman smiled. She had auburn hair pulled into a loose ponytail and green eyes that seemed to take in everything without judgment. "Couldn't sleep. Thought I'd come see the new guy." She looked at Nate. "You're Nate, right?"

"Yeah. How did you—"

"David mentioned you. Said you were doing okay for someone who didn't know a two top from a coffee cup."

Rebecca made a sound that might've been a laugh. "He's learning."

Sophie extended her hand. "Sophie Moreau. I work concierge and guest services. Basically, if someone needs something, I make it happen."

Nate shook her hand. Her grip was warm and confident. "Nice to meet you."

"Likewise." She glanced at Rebecca. "Mind if I steal him for a few minutes? I need an extra set of hands upstairs."

Rebecca looked at Nate, then at the dining room. Most of the breakfast guests had left. The few remaining were reading newspapers or working on laptops. "Fine. But I want him back by ten."

"Deal." Sophie gestured for Nate to follow. "Come on. I'll show you the fun part of the job."

They took the elevator to the sixth floor. Sophie used a key card that she wore on a lanyard around her neck. The elevator was smooth and silent, and Nate noticed there were buttons for floors one through nine, but the button for ten was covered with a small metal plate that had a keyhole in it.

"What's on ten?" he asked.

Sophie glanced at the button. "Above your pay grade. And mine, honestly. I've been here three years and I've never been up there."

"What do you think is up there?"

"I try not to think about it." She said it lightly, but there was something in her tone that suggested she meant it. "Best advice I can give you? Don't get curious about things you're not supposed to know. It's not worth it."

The elevator doors opened onto a hallway identical to the ones Nate had seen earlier. Sophie led him to Room 614 and knocked.

"Housekeeping," she called.

No response.

She unlocked the door and stepped inside. The room was a suite—a living area with a couch and chairs, a separate bedroom visible through an open doorway. On the coffee table sat a laptop, an empty coffee cup, and a leather portfolio embossed with a logo Nate recognized from somewhere but couldn't place.

Sophie picked up a notepad from the sideboard. "Guest requested extra towels, a bottle of Macallan 18, and a secure line installed for a conference call at two p.m. The towels are easy. The scotch is in the cellar. The phone line is your job."

"I don't know how to install a secure line."

"You're going to learn. Come on."

They took the service stairs down to the basement. It was cooler down here, and the walls were exposed brick instead of plaster. Sophie led him through a maze of hallways to a room marked "Telecom."

Inside was a workbench covered with cables, phone equipment, and tools that looked like they ranged from last week to last century. A man in his fifties sat at the bench, soldering something that sparked when he touched it.

"Martin," Sophie said. "This is Nate. He needs to learn how to set up a secure line."

Martin looked up. He had thick glasses and hands that looked too big for the delicate work he was doing. "Another one?"

"Another one."

Martin sighed and set down his soldering iron. "All right, kid. Pay attention. I'm only showing you this once."

He spent the next twenty minutes walking Nate through a process that involved running a dedicated line from the basement to the sixth floor, installing a junction box, and configuring a phone that looked like it belonged in a spy movie. Nate tried to keep up, taking mental notes, knowing he'd probably forgotten half of it already.

When they got back to Room 614, Martin handed Nate the phone. "You install it. I'll watch."

Nate's hands were sweating. He found the junction box on the wall behind the desk and opened it. Three sets of terminals. Red, black, green. He attached the phone line to the red terminals—

"Wrong," Martin said.

Nate stopped. "What?"

"Secure line goes to black. Red is standard. Green is emergency."

Nate switched the connection. The phone clicked into place. He picked up the handset and heard a dial tone.

"Good," Martin said. "Now test it."

"Test it how?"

Martin pulled out a small device that looked like a voltage meter and attached it to the line. He nodded. "Clean. No bleed, no interference. You did it right."

It was the second time today someone had suggested Nate hadn't completely screwed up, and it felt better than it should have.

By the time they finished, it was nine fifty-five. Sophie walked him back to the restaurant.

"You did good," she said. "Martin doesn't usually let new hires touch anything on the first try."

"I nearly wired it wrong."

"But you didn't. And when he corrected you, you didn't argue or make excuses. That counts for a lot here." She stopped outside the restaurant doors. "Look, I know David and Rebecca are hard on you. That's how they are with everyone. But they're good at what they do, and if you pay attention, you'll learn more in two weeks than you would in two years somewhere else."

"Thanks."

"Also," Sophie added, "if you need help, ask me. I mean it. David and Rebecca are great, but they're not exactly the warm and fuzzy types. I've been where you are. I know it's overwhelming."

"It is."

She smiled. "It gets easier. I promise."

Nate watched her walk away and realized that for the first time since he'd walked into the Hotel that morning, he felt like maybe he could actually do this job.

Then he pushed through the restaurant doors and found Rebecca waiting for him with an expression that suggested the easy part of his day was over.

"Table nine needs to be reset. Table six has a stain on the tablecloth. And you've got silverware to polish. Get to work."

Nate got to work.

By the time his shift ended at two p.m., his feet were screaming, his back ached, and he'd developed a newfound respect for anyone who'd ever worked in hospitality. David met him at the front desk as he was getting ready to leave.

"Tomorrow, five-fifty at the door," David said.

"I'll be there."

"We'll see."

Nate walked out into the afternoon sunlight and realized he'd survived his first day. Barely. But he'd survived.

His phone buzzed. A text from an unknown number.

You did good today. See you tomorrow. —Sophie

He smiled and pocketed the phone.

Tomorrow, he thought. He could do this.

He had no idea how much harder it was going to get.
